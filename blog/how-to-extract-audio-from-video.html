How audio extraction works

An MP4 file is actually a container ‚Äî it holds multiple streams inside a single file: a video stream (usually encoded with H.264 or H.265) and an audio stream (usually AAC or MP3). Extracting audio means separating the audio stream from the container and saving it as a standalone audio file.

There are two approaches to this extraction:

Stream copy (lossless)

The audio stream is pulled out of the MP4 container without any re-encoding. This is the fastest method and produces no quality loss ‚Äî you get the exact audio data that was in the video, just in a different container (e.g. MP3 or M4A). This is how TurboConvert works.

Re-encoding

The audio is decoded and re-encoded to a new format, which allows format conversion but introduces a small amount of quality loss (even at high bitrates). Some tools do this unnecessarily, which is why using a stream copy method is always preferable when quality matters.

Does extraction affect audio quality?

If the tool uses stream copy (as TurboConvert does), the audio quality is identical to what was in the original video. You are not compressing or re-encoding anything ‚Äî just separating the audio from the video container.

If the tool re-encodes the audio, you will lose a small amount of quality. The difference is rarely audible at bitrates above 128 kbps, but for professional audio work, always prefer a tool that does lossless extraction.

The quality of the extracted MP3 ultimately depends on what quality was used when the original video was recorded or exported. If the source video has poor audio, the extraction will reflect that.
