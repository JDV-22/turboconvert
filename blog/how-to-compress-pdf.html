Why are PDFs so large?

A PDF can be large for several reasons. The most common is embedded images ‚Äî when you export a presentation or scan a document, images are often stored at full resolution (300 DPI or higher). A single high-resolution page can represent several megabytes of image data.

Other contributors include embedded fonts (PDFs often bundle the full font file), metadata, annotations, and revision history left over from editing software like Adobe Acrobat or Word.

What compression actually does

When you compress a PDF, the tool essentially rewrites the file more efficiently. It does two things:

  • Lossless optimisation ‚Äî removes redundant metadata, duplicate objects, and wasted whitespace in the file structure. This typically saves 5‚Äì20% regardless of content.
  • Image re-encoding ‚Äî reduces the resolution of embedded images (e.g. from 300 DPI to 150 DPI). This is where the big savings come from ‚Äî 40‚Äì80% reduction for image-heavy files.

Text and vector graphics (lines, shapes, diagrams) are never affected by compression. They remain perfectly sharp because they are stored as mathematical instructions, not pixels.

Does compression affect quality?

It depends on what you mean by quality. Text will never be degraded ‚Äî it remains perfectly readable at any compression level. Images will be slightly softer at high compression, but for screen viewing and email sharing, the difference is imperceptible.

For print-ready files, use a higher quality setting (150‚Äì300 DPI). For email attachments or web uploads, maximum compression (72 DPI) is perfectly fine.

Compression levels explained

Maximum compression (72 DPI)

Images are re-encoded at 72 DPI ‚Äî the standard screen resolution. File sizes are smallest. Best for emailing, uploading to portals, or sharing on messaging apps. Not suitable for printing.

Balanced (150 DPI)

The sweet spot. Images are still clearly readable, file size is significantly reduced, and the result is suitable for most professional uses. This is the recommended default.

High quality (300 DPI)

Minimal image quality loss. Best for documents that will be printed or archived. File size reduction is modest (10‚Äì30%).

Tips for best results

  • Start with Balanced. It gives the best ratio of size reduction to quality. Switch to Maximum only if the file is still too large.
  • Compress before sending, not after. If you compress an already-compressed file, you lose quality without gaining much in size.
  • Scanned PDFs compress the most. A 20-page scanned contract at 10 MB can often be reduced to under 2 MB at Balanced quality.
  • Presentations compress well too. PowerPoint-exported PDFs often contain large images. Expect 50‚Äì70% reduction.
  • Password-protected PDFs cannot be compressed without first removing the password.
