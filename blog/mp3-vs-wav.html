The basics: what each format is

WAV (Waveform Audio File Format)

WAV is an uncompressed audio format developed by Microsoft and IBM in 1991. It stores raw audio data ‚Äî every single sample of the sound wave ‚Äî with no quality loss. Think of it as the digital equivalent of a master recording tape: perfect quality, but large.

WAV files are the standard in professional audio: recording studios, broadcast, sound design, and DAWs (digital audio workstations) like Pro Tools, Logic Pro, and Ableton all use WAV as their native format.

MP3 (MPEG-1 Audio Layer III)

MP3 is a compressed audio format developed in the early 1990s by the Fraunhofer Society. It uses "psychoacoustic" compression: it analyses the sound and discards frequencies that the human ear is least likely to notice. The result is a file that's 5‚Äì10√ó smaller than WAV, with minimal perceptible quality loss at high bitrates.

MP3 is the most widely supported audio format in the world, compatible with every device, platform, and streaming service.

Sound quality compared

WAV is lossless ‚Äî it contains 100% of the original audio data. There is no such thing as a "higher quality" WAV: all WAV files at the same sample rate and bit depth are identical in quality.

MP3 quality depends on the bitrate:

  • 128 kbps ‚Äî standard quality. Audible compression artifacts on music with high frequencies. Fine for speech and podcasts.
  • 192 kbps ‚Äî good quality. Most people cannot distinguish this from the original on typical speakers or earphones.
  • 256 kbps ‚Äî very good quality. Indistinguishable from the original for most listeners in most conditions.
  • 320 kbps ‚Äî maximum MP3 bitrate. Essentially transparent ‚Äî the compression is imperceptible to the vast majority of listeners.

File size compared

A 3-minute stereo audio track at CD quality (44.1 kHz, 16-bit) occupies approximately:

  • WAV: ~30‚Äì32 MB
  • MP3 at 320 kbps: ~7 MB
  • MP3 at 192 kbps: ~4.5 MB
  • MP3 at 128 kbps: ~3 MB

For a full album of 12 tracks, the difference is roughly 350 MB (WAV) versus 80 MB (MP3 at 320 kbps). At scale ‚Äî a music library of thousands of songs ‚Äî this difference is enormous.

When to use each format

Use WAV when:

  • You are recording, mixing, or mastering audio in a DAW
  • You need to edit and re-export multiple times without quality degradation
  • You are submitting audio to a broadcaster, film studio, or professional client
  • You are archiving original recordings for long-term storage

Use MP3 when:

  • You are publishing a podcast or distributing music online
  • You need to share a large audio file via email, WhatsApp, or Google Drive
  • You are loading music onto a phone, car stereo, or media player
  • You are serving audio on a website and need fast loading times
